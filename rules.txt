**Part 1: Linguistic Rules of Cape Muslim Afrikaans**

**Phonological Rules**
*   The diphthong **ui** [oey] is pronounced as **ei** [ei] (e.g., *uiwe* becomes *eiwe*).
*   The past tense prefix **ge-** [xə] shifts to **ga-** [xa].
*   A double **d** or **t** located in the middle of a word is pronounced as **r** (e.g., *middag* becomes *marrag*, *bottel* becomes *borrel*).
*   Older 19th-century creolized Cape Dutch pronunciations are preserved, such as using *twalf* instead of standard Afrikaans *twaalf*.

**Morphological and Syntactical Rules**
*   **Base Syntax:** The language strictly uses the Indo-Germanic Subject-Object-Verb (SOV) order, allowing the blending of Dutch with Malayo-Polynesian and Arabic lexicons.
*   **Adverbs of Degree:** Created by combining an Arabic noun with the Afrikaans suffix **-ste** (e.g., *sahiehste*).
*   **Pluralization:** Foreign nouns are integrated using the Afrikaans suffix **-s** (e.g., *salaahs*, *boetas*) or diminutive **-tjie** (e.g., *boetatjies*).
*   **Abstract Nouns:** Formed by adding the Afrikaans suffix **-heid** or **-skap** to an Arabic/Malayo-Polynesian word (e.g., *soppangheid*, *warraiskap*).
*   **Nouns from Verbs:** Created by adding the Afrikaans suffix **-er** to a Malayo-Polynesian verb (e.g., *slamblie-er*).
*   **Adjectives:** Formed by attaching the Afrikaans suffix **-se** to foreign words (e.g., *djapandoeliese*).
*   **Past Tense Verbs (Morphological Integration):** The intensity of integration is marked by adding the Afrikaans past-tense prefix **ge-** to Arabic or Malayo-Polynesian root verbs (e.g., *gesouwe*, *gelamba*, *ge-ghoesl*, *ge-batcha*, *genieyat*).
*   **Composite Syntactical Reversal:** When building composite words, the language discards the traditional Arabic/Malayo-Polynesian Noun-Adjective order, reversing it to fit the Afrikaans Adjective-Noun order (e.g., *ouwal-moereeds* instead of *moereeds-ouwal*, *asal-niesaab* instead of *niesaab-asal*, *slamse skool*).
*   **Functional Dualism:** Speakers use Malayo-Polynesian and Arabic equivalents for the same concept interchangeably based on nuance (e.g., using Malayu *abdas* for ablution, but Arabic *ghoesl* for the full bath).

**Orthographic and Transcription Rules (Arabic Script for Afrikaans)**
*   **Consonants:** 'b', 'd', 'f', 'h', 'j', 'k', 'l', 'm', 'n', 'r', 't' correspond to standard Arabic letters; 'g' (guttural) = *kha*; 'g' (hard) = modified *kaf* with an upper stroke; 'ng' = *kaf* shifted left; 'p' = *pe* (Persian); 's' = *sin* (except emphatic *sad* for *netsoes*); 'tj' = *che* (Persian); 'v' = *fa*; 'w' = *waw* or *fa*; 'z' is absent.
*   **Short Vowels:** 'a' = *fatha*; 'i' = *kasra*; 'oe' = *damma* + *waw*; 'o' = *fatha* + *damma*.
*   **Long Vowels & Diphthongs:** 'aa' = *fatha* + *alif*; 'ie' = *kasra* + *ya*; 'e' = *fatha* + *kasra*; 'eu' = *fatha* + *kasra* + *waw*; 'ai' = *fatha* + *ya*; 'ei' = *fatha* + *kasra* + *ya*; 'au' = *fatha* + *waw*; 'oei' = *waw* + *ya*; 'aai' = *fatha* + *alif* + *ya* + *hamza*. Initial vowels must use an *alif* carrier.
*   **Punctuation & Grammar Marks:** *Sukun* is used for vowelless consonants, *tashdid* (doubling) is entirely ignored, Arabic noun endings (*tanwin/ta'marbuta*) are discarded, numerals are spelled out, and capitalization does not exist. A horizontal dash marks a full stop, a double colon separates languages, and a four-dot diamond marks paragraphs.

### Part 1: Linguistic and Syntactical Rules Missed

**1. Intensity of Integration via Morphemes**
The strength of bridging an Arabic or Malayo-Polynesian loanword into Cape Muslim Afrikaans is determined by its grammatical usage. Affixing Afrikaans grammatical morphemes (like adding *-s* for plurals, *ge-* for past tense, or *-heid/-skap* for abstract nouns) indicates how intensely a foreign word has been integrated into the dialect.

**2. Expanded Composite Syntax Rule (Syntactical Reversal)**
When creating bilingual composites, the language completely discards the traditional Arabic/Malayo-Polynesian Noun-Adjective order. Instead, it strictly enforces the Afrikaans Adjective-Noun order. For example, the Arabic "original taxable property" (*niesaab-asal*) and "necessary tax" (*niesaab-waajieb*) are forcibly reversed to **asal-niesaab** and **waajieb-niesaab** to match Afrikaans syntax.

**3. The Base Syntactical Rule (SOV Framework)**
The language strictly utilizes the Indo-Germanic Dutch/Afrikaans Subject-Object-Verb (SOV) word order. This framework acts as the common denominator allowing the seamless blending of Dutch with Malayo-Polynesian languages.

**4. The Malayo-Polynesian Lexical Inheritance Rule**
To supplement the limitations of its 19th-century Cape Dutch core vocabulary, the dialect directly brings in inherited words from the ancestral slave languages (Sanskrit, Sunda, Malayu) rather than translating them.

**5. The Arabic Lexical Borrowing Rule**
Arabic words, specifically regarding religion, are never translated into Afrikaans. The original Arabic keywords are wholly preserved as fundamental concepts (e.g., using *masiet* and *Qiblah* instead of Dutch equivalents).

**6. Phonetic Creolization (Orthoepic Rules)**
The language deliberately preserves older, 19th-century creolized Cape Dutch pronunciations that never made it into Standard Afrikaans (such as writing and saying *twalf* instead of *twaalf*).
